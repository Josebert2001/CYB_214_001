For this practical, the main things you should recognize are:
**Arduino Uno**, **push button**, **breadboard**, **LED**, and a **current-limiting resistor**. Arduino’s button examples show that a pushbutton simply connects two points when pressed, and Arduino’s LED examples use a resistor in series with the LED to protect it. Arduino also documents `INPUT_PULLUP`, which is very useful for stable button reading. ([Arduino][1])

Here is how to know the **correct parts**:

**1. Arduino Uno**
This is the main blue board with digital pins at the top and power/analog pins at the bottom. It is the controller for the whole circuit.

**2. Push button**
The common one for this practical is the small **6×6 mm tactile push button**. It is a **momentary** switch, meaning it only connects while you are pressing it. ([Arduino][1])

**3. Breadboard**
This is the white board with many holes used to build the circuit without soldering.

**4. LED**
The LED has polarity. The **longer leg is the anode (+)** and the **shorter leg is the cathode (-)**. If the legs were cut, the **flat side** on the LED body usually marks the cathode. ([makecode.adafruit.com][2])

**5. Resistor**
Use a **220 Ω resistor** or something close in that range for the LED in a simple 5V Arduino circuit. Arduino’s LED tutorial uses a resistor in series with the external LED. ([Arduino][3])

This is the **simple practical picture in your head**:

* the **button** is the input
* the **Arduino** reads whether it is pressed
* the **LED** is the output
* the **resistor** protects the LED
* the **breadboard** holds the connections together ([Arduino][1])

A very safe way to wire this practical is:

* **button → digital pin 2 and GND**
* set pin 2 as **INPUT_PULLUP**
* **LED anode (+) → digital output pin**
* **LED cathode (-) → resistor → GND** ([Arduino Documentation][4])

So for **Practical 1**, the correct things to physically pick out from your components are:

* 1 Arduino Uno
* 1 small tactile push button
* 1 LED
* 1 resistor around 220 Ω
* jumper wires
* breadboard ([Arduino][1])
